Senior Capstone Project

Compact Magnetic Sensor

My team, consisting of James Li, Sean Barich, Sam Solondz, Panupoan (Touzong) Xiong, and myself, are partnering with Creative Magnetism Solutions, LLC. to build a compact, portable, low-cost magnetic field sensor. The device is waterproof and has a battery life of at least one week, allowing it to be left in remote areas. The device can measure the magnetic field with an accuracy of 10nT in three axes, and reports collected data to an Android phone via Bluetooth. We also designed an Android app for visualization of the magnetic field and easy use of data later.

The Overall Design

The magnetic sensor we chose was the HMC1001 and HMC1002 for X&Y and Z axes, respectively. This sensor outputs a differential voltage, with a theoretical resolution of about 3nT. We used a differential, external ADC to convert the differential voltage into a numerical value for storage in the microcontroller. Because the ADC only had one differential input we used a 3 to 1 multiplexer to sequentially sample each axis, which also decreases cross-talk between lines. We used the BGM111 as our microcontroller because it has a built-in Bluetooth antenna and Bluetooth Low Energy capability. Transmission of magnetic sensor data occurs over BLE and is graphed in real time by an Android application, as well as being saved for use later.

I was in charge of designing and testing the power system and designing and laying out the PCB. Firmware, ADC selection and circuitry, magnetic sensor circuitry, and the Android app were handled by my teammates.

The Power System

Three steady voltage rails were required: 3.3V for the microcontroller, 5V for the ADC and magnetic sensor, and 8V for a set/reset function in the magnetic sensor. I decided to use three 18650 lithium ion cells in series to achieve a nominal battery voltage of 11.1V. Because lithium ion batteries are rechargable, this would also decrease the lifetime cost of the device, and the standard lithium ion cell size meant that the batteries themselves are easy and cheap to find. To achieve the three voltage rails required, I used three switching buck converters from TI that have very high efficiency and an automatic power-saving low-current mode. This produced three steady voltage rails to power the system.

PCB Design

The final PCB was two 4-layer boards with signal planes on the top and bottom and two internal ground planes. This stackup was chosen to minimize noise by minimizing the signal's distance to ground. Because the fluctuations in the magnetic field produced voltage changes on the scale of millivolts, any noise from the buck converters, microcontroller, or ADC could potentially spoil the readings from the magnetic sensor. Thus, the magnetic sensor was placed on its own, separate board, which was connected using ribbon cables to the main board, which contained all other components and systems.
